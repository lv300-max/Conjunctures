Landau's 4th Problem Engine

Are there infinitely many primes of the form n²+1?
Asked by Landau in 1912. Unanswered for over 110 years.
This engine computes a finite certificate — every n²+1 that is prime up to N — measures the density constant, documents the weakest witness, and maps exactly where the proof wall stands. The wall has a name: the Parity Problem.
P(N) = #{n ≤ N : n²+1 is prime}  |  density d = P(N) / (N / log(N))  |  Expected by Hardy-Littlewood: P(N) ~ C · N / log(N), C ≈ 1.3727...
⚠ THE PARITY PROBLEM — THE KNOWN HARD WALL
All classical sieve methods (Brun, Selberg, large sieve) fail to prove infinitely many primes of the form n²+1. The reason: sieves cannot distinguish between numbers with an even vs odd number of prime factors. To count primes in a quadratic sequence, you need to break this parity barrier — and no one has.

Best known result (Iwaniec, 1978): There are infinitely many n such that n²+1 has at most 2 prime factors. That is the frontier. From "at most 2 factors" to "exactly 1 factor (prime)" is the open gap.
